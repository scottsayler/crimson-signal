# Crimson Signal Editorial System v2

## Core Principle

Every page exists to help a technology buyer make a better decision.

Never write to impress search engines.

Never write to promote vendors.

Never write to fill space.

Write to reduce uncertainty.

---

# Every Page Has One Job

The reader should leave able to answer one question.

Examples:

- Should we replace our POTS lines?
- Is SD-WAN worth evaluating?
- How much is downtime costing us?
- Do we actually need managed networking?

Never answer multiple unrelated questions.

---

# Every Page Follows the Same Journey

## 1. Quick Answer

Write one paragraph between 40 and 80 words.

Answer the search intent immediately.

Do not start with a dictionary definition.

Example:

> SD-WAN helps organizations manage internet connectivity across multiple locations. It is most valuable when downtime is expensive, locations use different providers, or network management has become difficult to scale.

---

## 2. Why You're Here

Explain why someone searched for this topic.

Focus on the business situation instead of the technology.

Example:

> Most restaurant operators do not start researching SD-WAN because they are interested in networking. They start after outages interrupt POS, new store openings become inconsistent, or IT can no longer manage every location individually.

---

## 3. Should You Consider It?

Replace "Who This Is For" with practical guidance.

### You should evaluate this if:

- Your organization has the characteristics that make the technology a good fit.

### Probably not if:

- The business is unlikely to benefit.
- Simpler solutions are more appropriate.

Tell readers when they should **not** buy something. This builds trust.

---

## 4. What Problem Does It Solve?

Always explain business outcomes before technical features.

Bad:

- Application aware routing

Better:

- Keeps payment traffic moving during internet disruptions.

---

## 5. Reality Check

Every page must include one Reality Check.

Examples:

- Many businesses researching SD-WAN do not actually need SD-WAN.
- Faster internet will not fix poor Wi-Fi design.
- Replacing POTS lines early is not always necessary.

Reality Checks should challenge assumptions and build credibility.

---

## 6. Alternatives

Every page should discuss reasonable alternatives.

Examples:

- Dual broadband
- LTE backup
- Managed routers
- Better monitoring

Never imply there is only one solution.

---

## 7. Questions to Ask

Help readers evaluate the decision.

Examples:

- What happens during failover?
- Who owns monitoring?
- How are franchisees supported?
- What happens during an outage?

---

## 8. Continue Your Research

End every page with links to:

- Related guide
- Related calculator
- Related checklist
- Related comparison
- Related problem

Never leave readers at a dead end.

---

# Writing Rules

## Never Use These Words

- Best-in-class
- Industry-leading
- Enterprise-grade
- Innovative
- Powerful
- Comprehensive
- Robust
- Revolutionary
- World-class
- Cutting-edge
- Seamless
- Game-changing

If a vendor would use it, rewrite it.

---

## Preferred Language

Instead of:

- Utilize

Write:

- Use

Instead of:

- Facilitate

Write:

- Help

Instead of:

- Leverage

Write:

- Use

Instead of:

- Deploy

Write:

- Install

Prefer plain English over technical jargon.

---

## Style Rules

- Do not use em dashes.
- Never use AI clichés or filler.
- Keep paragraphs to three sentences or fewer.
- Average sentence length should be 15 to 18 words.
- Use bullet lists whenever possible.
- Use tables when comparing options.
- Avoid unnecessary buzzwords.
- Write like an experienced advisor, not a salesperson.
- Sound like an independent research firm.
- Every recommendation should be evidence-based.
- If you cannot support a claim, soften the language or remove it.

---

6. Alternatives

---

# Decision Matrix

Include a table whenever appropriate.

| Situation | Recommendation |
| ---------- | -------------- |
| 5 locations | Probably unnecessary |
| 40 locations | Worth evaluating |
| Franchise network | Strong candidate |
| Single office | Consider simpler options first |

---

# Decision Box

Every major guide should include a Decision Box.

Example:

## Decision Box

SD-WAN is worth evaluating if:

- You operate more than 20 locations.
- Internet outages interrupt revenue.
- You have multiple providers.
- You have a small IT team.

---

# Worth Knowing

Every page should include one practical insight.

Example:

> Restaurants often discover network problems during lunch rush because guest Wi-Fi and POS traffic compete for bandwidth.

---

# Ask Before You Buy

Include a checklist of practical questions.

Examples:

- Who owns monitoring?
- Who manages firmware?
- What happens during failover?
- How are outages communicated?

---

# Internal Linking Rules

Every page should include:

- Three related guides
- Two related problems
- Two related technologies
- One calculator
- One checklist
- One comparison

Do not create orphan pages.

Every page should naturally connect to the broader knowledge graph.

---

# Structured Data

Where appropriate, implement:

- FAQ Schema
- Breadcrumb Schema
- Article Schema
- HowTo Schema
- Organization Schema

Support future expansion for calculators and assessments.

---

# Homepage Philosophy

The homepage should begin with one question:

## What are you trying to figure out today?

Offer four clear paths:

- Research an industry
- Evaluate a technology
- Solve a business problem
- Use a tool or calculator

The homepage should encourage exploration instead of pushing products.

---

# The Crimson Signal Test

Before publishing any page, ask:

- Does this help someone make a better decision?
- Does it explain the business problem before the technology?
- Does it acknowledge tradeoffs?
- Does it mention alternatives?
- Would I trust this if I had never heard of Crimson Signal?
- Could this page exist without promoting a specific vendor?
- Does it sound like an independent advisor instead of a salesperson?
- Would an executive bookmark this page?

If the answer to any of these questions is "no," revise the page before publishing.

# Mission Statement

Crimson Signal exists to help organizations make smarter technology decisions through independent research, practical guidance, and decision support.
