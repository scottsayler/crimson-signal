maturityObservations:
  - Organizations with fewer than five locations rarely have a formal location technology blueprint — standards emerge reactively after inconsistencies surface.
  - Mature organizations define a non-negotiable clinical technology stack before site 3 opens; immature organizations standardize after site 10.
  - HIPAA compliance maturity correlates with centralized BAA inventory management — not with security tool deployment.
  - Telehealth readiness is often treated as a feature rollout rather than a cutover dependency, delaying revenue at new sites.
  - High-maturity organizations track facilities milestones and technology readiness on a single plan with shared ownership.
operationalPatterns:
  - Clinical operations and IT use different definitions of ready-to-open — the gap surfaces at the first patient encounter.
  - Medical device networking is scoped separately from administrative IT, creating late-stage compliance gaps.
  - Local vendor contracts signed during opening constrain enterprise architecture for years.
  - EHR cutover windows constrain opening timelines more than WAN provisioning in most rollouts.
  - Security scope expands with every site, but remediation is deferred until audit pressure or incident exposure.

## Context

These ranges reflect recurring observations across multi-site healthcare organizations — not survey-derived precision data. Actual timelines vary by organization size, EHR complexity, and whether a location blueprint exists before expansion accelerates.

Organizations opening fewer than three sites per year can often absorb bespoke approaches. Above that cadence, the cost of non-standardization compounds faster than leadership teams typically model.
