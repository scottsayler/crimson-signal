  executiveObservation: >-
    In restaurants, new locations must deliver consistent guest experience from the first
    service — kitchen systems, POS, and connectivity failures are immediately visible to
    customers and staff. The strategic intent is uniform standards across inherited sites
    — but acquired and franchised locations rarely arrive with compatible architecture.
    The tension is not whether to standardize; it is how much heterogeneity must be
    absorbed before the blueprint can take hold. Guest experience pressure at this stage
    usually signals that inconsistency is already visible in the market — not a future
    risk. Leadership should treat opening readiness as an end-to-end service test —
    kitchen, POS, payments, and delivery channels integrated — not a facilities handoff
    with technology to follow.
  whatWeHeard:
    - Growth through acquisition or franchising will inherit heterogeneous technology estates — early standardization decisions determine how much integration work follows.
    - A rollout of 6–20 locations across 3–12 months demands repeatable deployment — bespoke approaches at each site will not sustain the cadence.
    - Full standardization across sites is the ambition — which raises the bar for blueprint definition, testing, and governance before the first opening.
    - Guest experience consistency is where leadership attention is focused — the technology implications of that priority deserve explicit ownership before commitments are made.
  likelyImpacts:
    - Inherited technology from acquired or franchised sites will require explicit rationalization — deferring that work multiplies support cost and security exposure.
    - Kitchen display, POS, and payment systems must be synchronized before the first service — partial deployments create order errors and guest complaints.
    - Franchise and multi-brand models introduce technology variation that conflicts with menu, pricing, and loyalty program consistency.
    - Guest Wi-Fi, online ordering, and delivery platform integrations are often treated as add-ons rather than opening-day requirements.
    - Operational tooling designed for a single campus does not automatically support provisioning, monitoring, and supporting a distributed footprint.
  blindSpots:
    - Back-of-house networking is frequently under-scoped compared to front-of-house POS, leaving kitchen systems on fragile connectivity.
    - Labor scheduling and tip pooling systems are often provisioned after POS go-live, creating payroll and compliance gaps in early weeks.
    - Business milestones and technology readiness are rarely tracked on the same plan.
    - Total technology cost by location is frequently unknown until renewal cycles — after commitments are already locked in.
  questionsToExplore:
    - Can this location run a full service — dine-in, takeout, and delivery — on opening day without manual workarounds?
    - Which menu, pricing, and loyalty configurations must be identical to existing locations versus locally adapted?
    - Is there a documented location playbook, or does each site inherit the preferences of whoever managed the last opening?
    - Who owns the technology implications of opening new locations — and do they have visibility across the full footprint?
